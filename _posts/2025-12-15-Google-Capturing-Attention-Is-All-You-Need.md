## Introduction

Advertising has long been the lifeblood of the internet economy. Platforms like Google and Amazon perfected a model where users’ attention is the product, and advertisers pay for placement. Google earns over 80% of its revenue from ads, primarily through pay-per-click (PPC) search placements and YouTube display/video ads. Amazon, meanwhile, leverages its marketplace to sell “sponsored products,” with advertisers paying for prime visibility on product searches.

In both cases, the distribution of money is straightforward: advertisers pay the platform, the platform takes the majority of the margin, and users see specific ads with little or no share of that value.

## The Current Ad Model

In the traditional setup:

- **Advertisers** pay platforms for visibility (bidding in auctions for search results, product placement, or social media feeds).
- **Platforms** (Google, Amazon, Meta, etc.) act as gatekeepers, controlling access to audiences and taking a large cut of ad spend.
- **Users** provide attention and data, but capture none of the direct economic value.

The platforms maximize profits by scaling impressions, clicks, and conversions. This creates high-margin businesses: for example, Google Search ads alone have operating margins of 30–40% because they capture nearly all of the advertiser’s spend.

## How I Think Ads Will Work in the Agent Era

In a recent podcast appearance, Aravind Srinivas (CEO of Perplexity) envisions a different world where AI agents become the new intermediaries between brands and consumers. Imagine telling your agent: “Book me a ride to JFK.” Instead of you scrolling through Uber and Lyft, your agent negotiates with their agents.

Advertisers and brands will no longer target you directly. Instead, they will present offers to **your agent**.

Your agent becomes the filter, negotiating for your preferences (cheapest, most sustainable, fastest, or even loyalty perks).

This changes revenue distribution:

- A portion of advertiser spend goes to the **agent platform** (e.g., Perplexity, OpenAI, Anthropic).
- A portion may go **directly to you** as a rebate, discount, or credit, since your agent acts in your economic interest.

The advertiser still gets the sale, but without relying on manipulative clicks or irrelevant impressions.

**Margins compress.** Instead of Google pocketing nearly all ad dollars, the money is split between:

- The **agent company** (for infrastructure, compute, trust)—unlocking a different scope of agentic attention capture.
- The **user’s agent** (for providing attention and transaction opportunities).
- The **advertiser’s agent** (potentially paying lower acquisition costs due to precision targeting).

## Why This Disrupts Google’s Model

This system undermines Google’s search ad monopoly. Today, advertisers pay billions for visibility bidding to appear on the first page of results. In the agent world, there’s no first page.

There was a joke: “If you want to hide a dead body, hide it on the fourth page of Google search”—no one visits it.

There’s a **negotiation**.

If AI agents handle even 30–40% of consumer decisions, Google could lose a significant portion of its $175B annual ad revenue, as its entire model depends on controlling discovery. Instead of consumers being nudged by “sponsored results,” their agents will filter and negotiate ads on their behalf, forcing platforms to share value with users. A whole new game of wooing the agents will come into the picture.

I respect Google because they saw it coming and **disrupted** their existing business model with AI mode, after Perplexity was challenging them—self-disruption.

Let’s see what steps Google is taking.

## Google’s Countersteps: A2A, AP2, and x402

Google is not blind to this disruption. It has already begun laying the groundwork for an agent-first economy through open protocols:

- **A2A (Agent-to-Agent)** — A protocol for interoperability, allowing different AI agents to talk to each other securely. If Google’s standard becomes dominant, it ensures Google remains the backbone of agent ecosystems.
- **AP2 (Agent Payments Protocol)** — A new standard for enabling purchases through agents, with cryptographically signed “mandates” so transactions are trustworthy. This ensures that agents can complete the last mile of commerce, not just discovery.
- **x402** — An extension within AP2 that allows payments to flow via stablecoins, blockchains, and alternative rails. It’s based on HTTP 402 (“Payment Required”) and enables low-cost, programmable payments.

In short, while companies like Perplexity are redefining the interface, Google is working on the **infrastructure**. If agents become the future of the internet, Google wants to own the rails—the pipes through which money and data flow.

This is a classic **Box 1 + Box 2** defense: protect existing revenues while building protocols to avoid being sidelined. But it doesn’t fully answer the **Box 3** question: how will Google create the future?

## What Google Is Not Doing (Yet)

Despite its infrastructure play, Google still has gaps:

- **User incentives:** No clear strategy to share ad value or transaction rebates directly with users, which competitors may use as a wedge.
- **Owning the agent interface:** Google hasn’t yet launched a mass-market “personal agent” as sticky and trusted as its Search once was. Gemini is powerful but not yet positioned as a daily-life agent—as of now.
- **Trust certification:** No framework for monetizing trust (e.g., verified recommendations) rather than clicks.

## Recommendations for Google

To truly protect its 70% ad revenue and prepare for a post-search world, Google should:

1. **Launch a consumer-facing agent** integrated deeply into Android, Chrome, and Search, ensuring users default to Google’s ecosystem.
2. **Share value with users** by turning ad spend into rebates, credits, or loyalty tokens, making users feel like partners in the system rather than products.
3. **Build a trust network** where brands pay not for visibility, but for verified credibility within Google’s agent ecosystem.
4. **Monetize protocols smartly:** Google is unlikely to take micro-fees on agent-to-agent protocols because pricing the base layer introduces friction. Instead, it would keep the protocol free to drive adoption and monetize adjacent layers like infrastructure, tooling, or orchestration.

## Conclusion

The future of advertising looks less like banners and search results, and more like agent-to-agent negotiations. Users will gain a share of ad value, advertisers will compete on relevance rather than spend, and platforms will need to reinvent their business models.
